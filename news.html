DR Congo: A fragile truce amid imperialist exploitation and bloodshed

American leader's erratic approach to Ukraine and Middle East means challenges for stability-fixated Beijing amid its shifting Moscow ties
Reading Time: 6 minutes
A political cartoon depicting international tensions between major world powers with Trump and China's leader at the center of diplomatic struggles. (Illustration: Thomas Bowman)
Published: 11:00pm, 24 Apr 2025 | Updated: 1:06am, 25 Apr 2025

Three months into Donald Trump's return to the White House, Chinese officials are struggling to formulate a coherent response to what analysts describe as an increasingly unpredictable US foreign policy, particularly regarding Ukraine and the Middle East.

Beijing's diplomatic corps, accustomed to stability and methodical planning, has been thrown off balance by the rapid shifts in Washington's approach to global hotspots, according to multiple sources familiar with internal Chinese foreign policy discussions.

"The Chinese leadership values predictability above all else in foreign relations," said Zhang Wei, director of the Center for American Studies at Beijing University. "Trump's swift policy reversals on Ukraine funding, NATO commitments, and Middle East engagement have created significant challenges for Beijing's strategic planning."

Most concerning for China has been the evolving US-Russia relationship, which has shifted noticeably since Trump's inauguration in January. After years of deteriorating ties during the previous administration, Trump's apparent willingness to engage with Moscow without preconditions has complicated China's own carefully calibrated relationship with Russia.

"China has benefited enormously from Russia's isolation over the past several years," explained Sarah Johnson, a China-Russia relations expert at the International Crisis Group. "Beijing became Moscow's economic lifeline while maintaining enough distance to avoid secondary sanctions. Any US-Russia reconciliation threatens to undermine that advantageous position."

Chinese officials have reportedly accelerated plans for high-level meetings with their Russian counterparts, concerned that Moscow might be tempted by potential US overtures to reduce its dependence on Chinese economic support.

The White House's approach to Taiwan has been equally disorienting for Beijing. After initially alarming Chinese officials with several direct calls to Taiwan's president‚Äîbreaking with decades of diplomatic protocol‚ÄîTrump has more recently downplayed the importance of the island democracy in public statements.

"We're seeing a whiplash effect in Beijing," said Michael Chen, former State Department advisor on East Asian affairs. "One week, they're preparing response options for what they perceive as American provocations on Taiwan, the next they're analyzing cryptic statements suggesting the US might reduce its commitment to Taiwan's security."

This unpredictability extends to trade relations as well. Despite campaign promises to impose massive new tariffs on Chinese goods, the administration has thus far taken a more measured approach, focusing instead on targeted restrictions in strategic sectors like advanced semiconductors and artificial intelligence.

Li Feng, an economist at Shanghai's Fudan University, noted that "Chinese economic planners prepared for worst-case scenarios involving across-the-board tariffs of up to 60%. The more surgical approach actually being implemented has been harder to counter because it's less politically inflammatory but potentially more damaging to China's technology ambitions."

Trade negotiators from both countries have met twice since January, but sources familiar with the discussions describe them as exploratory rather than substantive. "The Chinese side keeps asking for clarity on US intentions and long-term goals," said one person briefed on the talks. "They're essentially being told that everything is subject to change based on the president's assessment of 'results.'"

Regional partners are closely watching how China navigates this new reality. Japan and South Korea have increased diplomatic engagement with Beijing, hedging against uncertainty in US policy. Meanwhile, Southeast Asian nations report more assertive Chinese outreach offering economic partnerships with "no political strings attached"‚Äîa clear contrast to the oscillating US approach.

"For decades, Chinese foreign policy operated on the assumption that while US policies might change between administrations, they generally followed predictable patterns," said Robert Garcia, who studies Chinese foreign policy at Stanford University. "What we're seeing now is Beijing struggling to develop new analytical frameworks for a US that appears to make significant policy shifts based on limited planning or institutional process."

The Chinese Foreign Ministry has publicly maintained that it seeks "stable and predictable relations with the United States," but privately, officials acknowledge they are reviewing core assumptions about how to engage with Washington in this new era.

As one Beijing-based diplomat from an Asian country observed: "China has always played a long game in foreign policy. The question now is whether they can successfully play a game where the other major player keeps changing the rules."
